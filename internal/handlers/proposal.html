Executive Summary

Why we're proposing this now.

Our marketplace is generating a growing volume of WhatsApp messages — order tracking, delivery questions, refund requests, vendor escalations. Today these are scattered across personal phones and shared accounts. We have a production-ready, in-house platform that consolidates all of it.

One inbox for the whole marketplace

Every customer message lands in a single real-time queue. The central support team triages, resolves, or escalates to the right vendor — no phones, no shared accounts, no lost conversations.

Bot handles the predictable 70%

Order status, delivery ETA, return policy, FAQs — all answered automatically by pulling from our existing orders & product APIs. Agents only get involved when the bot escalates.

Zero recurring SaaS cost

We pay Meta directly for messaging — no per-conversation markup from a BSP. The platform itself runs on infrastructure we already operate. The build cost is already sunk; only deployment effort remains.

Marketplace Use Cases

What this changes day-to-day on BANGLAB2BB2C.

Concrete scenarios from a multivendor marketplace, mapped to what the platform already does. Nothing below is hypothetical — every flow is buildable with the nodes that ship today.

"Where's my order?"

Customer messages the support number. Bot asks for order ID, hits our orders API, replies with live status, courier name, and tracking URL — under 5 seconds, no agent involved.

Refund / return requests

Bot captures the order ID, reason, photo evidence. Creates a return ticket in the marketplace backend via webhook, and routes the conversation to the platform's refund agent only when paperwork is complete.

Vendor-specific escalations

Customer asks something only the vendor can answer (stock, customisation, bulk pricing). Agent attaches the order, generates a vendor-side ticket, and replies once vendor responds — all without leaving the conversation.

Delivery exception nudges

Courier marks an order "delivery failed". Marketplace fires a webhook → platform sends the customer a templated WhatsApp message with reschedule options as buttons. Customer reply triggers a new flow.

Abandoned-cart recovery

Cart inactive 6 hours? Send a soft nudge with the product image + a "complete checkout" CTA. Schedule a follow-up template 24 hours later if still unconverted.

Post-purchase NPS & reviews

Two days after delivery the platform asks "How was your order?" via a list-button rating. Low scores route to support; high scores prompt a product review link.

Payment & checkout help

"Payment failed" or "OTP not received" questions auto-trigger a flow that walks the customer through retry, alternate methods, or transfers to a payments agent if stuck.

Vendor onboarding queue

New vendor applications can use a separate WhatsApp number. Bot captures business name, KYC documents, contact details — agents only step in for verification calls.

Out-of-hours auto-responder

Outside the support team's working hours, bot sets expectations ("we'll reply at 9 AM tomorrow") and queues the conversation — no missed customers, no angry follow-ups.

Promotional campaigns

Segment customers by past purchases or vendor preference (data already in the marketplace DB), send a Meta-approved promo template to thousands at once with cost preview.

Append-only. Records who did what, when, from where, and the before/after diff for every mutation.

Runs entirely in your own infrastructure — VPC, on-prem, anywhere. Customer messages never traverse a third-party SaaS.

Marketplace Integration

How it plugs into our existing platform.

The support system runs as a peer service, not a fork of the marketplace. It talks to our existing backend over HTTP using a service-to-service token. No shared database, no migrations to coordinate.

Inbound from marketplace → support

Marketplace emits webhooks when interesting things happen — order placed, payment failed, delivery delayed, cart abandoned, vendor approved. Each webhook can trigger a templated WhatsApp message or kick off a chatbot flow.

Outbound from support → marketplace

The chatbot's "API Call" node lets a flow query our orders, products, vendors, or refund endpoints in real time and surface the answer to the customer. Reads are read-only; mutations (e.g. "raise refund") go through the same APIs your ops team already uses.

Single sign-on with the admin portal

Support agents log in with their existing marketplace admin credentials via OAuth/SSO. Roles map automatically: a marketplace admin becomes a support superadmin; ops staff become support agents.

Single WhatsApp Business number

We register one number (e.g. "BANGLAB2BB2C Support") under our Meta Business Account. All inbound traffic lands in one queue. Vendors keep using their personal phones if they want; nothing forces them onto WhatsApp.

Customer identity matching

On first message, the bot matches the WhatsApp number against our customers table. Recognised customer? Greet by name, list their recent orders. New number? Capture details and create a lead.

Vendor visibility

A dashboard widget in the existing vendor portal can show "open customer tickets" per vendor, pulled from this platform's API. Vendors stay informed without needing accounts in the support tool.

Deployment & Operations

One Docker image, one command.

The platform builds into a single Alpine-based Docker image. The Go binary embeds the compiled frontend, so there's no separate web server to manage — deployment is one docker compose up -d.

docker-compose.yml · 3 services ~ 30s to start 
services:
  app:
    image: banglab2bb2c:latest
    ports: ["8080:8080"]
    environment:
      BANGLAB2BB2C_DATABASE_HOST: db
      BANGLAB2BB2C_REDIS_HOST: redis
      BANGLAB2BB2C_APP_ENCRYPTION_KEY: ${ENC_KEY}
      BANGLAB2BB2C_JWT_SECRET: ${JWT}
    depends_on: [db, redis]
    restart: unless-stopped

  db:
    image: postgres:17-alpine
    volumes: [db-data:/var/lib/postgresql/data]

  redis:
    image: redis:7-alpine

# That's it. Migrations auto-run on boot.
# Default admin user is seeded from env vars.

Health monitoring

GET /health returns the build version + db / redis status. Wire into your monitoring of choice.

Coolify-ready

One-click Coolify deployment with automatic Let's Encrypt TLS via Traefik. Also works with Kubernetes, Nomad, or plain Docker.

Backups

PostgreSQL dump + Redis RDB snapshot are sufficient for a full restore. Daily off-site backups via the existing infrastructure pipeline.

Rollout Plan

From approval to live on WhatsApp in four phases.

The engineering work is done. What's left is operational — Meta verification, infrastructure, and team training. Realistic end-to-end timeline: 3-4 weeks, with most calendar time spent waiting on Meta's business verification.

1 Phase 1 — Meta Business setup  ·  Week 1

Submit our existing Meta Business account for verification (utility bill, trade license). Register the WhatsApp number that will host support conversations. Create the first message templates (order confirmation, delivery update, refund acknowledgement, abandoned-cart nudge).
Owners: Ops lead + finance for paperwork.

2 Phase 2 — Infrastructure & deploy  ·  Week 1-2 (parallel to phase 1)

Stand up a VPS (4 vCPU / 8 GB), provision PostgreSQL + Redis, point the marketplace's support.banglab2bb2c.com subdomain at it. Deploy the existing Docker image. Configure env vars from a vault. Smoke-test with internal numbers.
Owners: Engineering ·  ~2-3 working days.

3 Phase 3 — Marketplace integration  ·  Week 2

Wire the marketplace's existing webhooks to fire on order-placed, payment-failed, delivery-status-changed. Build the first three chatbot flows: "Where's my order?", Refund request, Out-of-hours auto-responder. Each flow calls back into our orders / refund APIs via the API Call node — no changes needed on the marketplace side beyond adding the webhook endpoints.
Owners: Engineering ·  ~3-4 working days.

4 Phase 4 — Team training & soft launch  ·  Week 3

Onboard the 3-5 person initial support team. Train on the inbox UI, canned responses, and the agent transfer flow. Soft-launch to a single city or a single vendor category. Monitor for one week, tune flows based on real conversations. Open the floodgates in week 4.
Owners: Ops lead + Engineering on standby.

Open Questions

Decisions we need leadership to make.

Should we start with one WhatsApp number or several?
Recommendation: start with one. A single number is simpler to brand, train agents on, and explain to customers. The platform already supports adding more numbers later — for example a separate vendor-onboarding number — without any rework.
Will vendors get any access to the support inbox?
Not in phase 1. All customer conversations stay with the central support team. What vendors will get is a notification (via the existing vendor portal) when one of their orders has an open support ticket, plus an internal note thread the support agent uses to ask the vendor for input. We can revisit vendor-side agent access later if needed.
Which message templates do we need on day one?
Minimum viable set: order confirmation, delivery dispatched, delivery delayed, refund initiated, and an abandoned-cart nudge. Templates need Meta approval (24-48h typically). The platform's template UI lets the ops team add more as the catalog matures.
What support hours are we committing to?
Recommendation: 9 AM - 9 PM Bangladesh time on day one. Bot auto-responds outside those hours with an ETA. As volume grows, extend to 24x7 by hiring a small night shift — the assignment engine already supports shift rotations.
How do we measure success after launch?
Three KPIs we should agree on up-front: (1) median first-response time (< 1 minute target), (2) bot self-service rate (target ~70% within 3 months), and (3) customer satisfaction via post-conversation NPS (target > 4.2 / 5). All three are visible on the analytics dashboard from day one.
What happens to existing support channels (email, phone)?
Recommendation: keep them. WhatsApp becomes the primary channel because that's where customers already are, but email and phone stay as backups. The audit log gives us six months of data to decide whether to deprecate any of them.
